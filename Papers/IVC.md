# MASTER STUDY NOTES: THE INDUS VALLEY CIVILIZATION (IVC)

**Target Exams:** UPSC Civil Services, State PSCs, SSC CGL, UGC-NET
(History), NDA/CDS.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND NOMENCLATURE

The Indus Valley Civilization (IVC) was a Bronze Age civilization in the
northwestern regions of South Asia, lasting from 3300 BCE to 1300 BCE,
and in its mature form from 2600 BCE to 1900 BCE. Together with ancient
Egypt and Mesopotamia, it was one of three early civilizations of the
Near East and South Asia, and of the three, the most widespread.

- **Also known as:**
  - **Harappan Civilization:** Named after Harappa, the first site
    excavated (1921). According to archaeological convention, an unknown
    civilization is named after its first discovered site.
  - **Indus-Saraswati Civilization:** Because a vast majority of
    settlements (over 80%) were found along the dried-up banks of the
    Ghaggar-Hakra River (believed to be the ancient Saraswati).
  - **Bronze Age Civilization:** They used bronze (an alloy of copper
    and tin), though copper was more prevalent.
  - **First Urbanization of India:** It represents the first phase of
    urban development in the Indian subcontinent (the second being
    during the Mahajanapada period, ~600 BCE).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. TIMELINE AND PHASES (Carbon-14 Dating)

The development of the Harappan Civilization is categorized into three
distinct phases:

1.  **Early Harappan Phase (3300 BCE – 2600 BCE):**
    - Formative, proto-urban phase.
    - Transition from rural to urban life.
    - Establishment of trade networks.
    - Important Sites: Amri, Kot Diji, Kalibangan (lower layers),
      Bhirrana (oldest site discovered).
2.  **Mature Harappan Phase (2600 BCE – 1900 BCE):**
    - *This is the phase most questions are asked about.*
    - Properly developed cities, burnt brick houses, standardized
      weights, and measures.
    - Full-fledged use of the Harappan script.
    - Important Sites: Harappa, Mohenjo-Daro, Lothal, Dholavira.
3.  **Late Harappan Phase (1900 BCE – 1300 BCE):**
    - Decline of urban features.
    - Return to rural/chalcolithic lifestyle.
    - Disappearance of the script, seals, and standard weights.
    - Also known as the **Cemetery H Culture** (found in Harappa) or
      **Jhukar/Jhangar Culture** (Sindh).

> **Exam Fact:** The radiocarbon (*C*<sub>14</sub>) dating proposed by
> D.P. Agrawal places the Mature Phase strictly between **2500 BCE and
> 1750 BCE**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 3. HISTORY OF DISCOVERY

- **1826:** Charles Masson (a British soldier/explorer) first noticed
  the ruins of Harappa.
- **1853 & 1873:** Sir Alexander Cunningham (First Director-General of
  ASI) visited Harappa and found a seal, but failed to grasp its
  antiquity, assuming it was Buddhist.
- **1921:** **Daya Ram Sahni** officially excavated Harappa under the
  direction of **Sir John Marshall** (then DG of ASI).
- **1922:** **R.D. Banerjee** excavated Mohenjo-Daro in Sindh.
- **1924:** Sir John Marshall officially announced the discovery of a
  new civilization in the Indus Valley to the world in the *Illustrated
  London News*.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 4. GEOGRAPHICAL EXTENT

The IVC was the largest of the ancient civilizations in terms of
geographical area (approx. 1.3 million sq. km). It covered parts of
modern-day Afghanistan, Pakistan, and Northwestern India.

**The Four Extreme Points (Highly asked in Prelims):** \* **Northernmost
Point:** **Manda** (Jammu & Kashmir) on the banks of the river Chenab.
\* **Southernmost Point:** **Daimabad** (Maharashtra) on the banks of
the river Pravara (a tributary of Godavari). \* **Easternmost Point:**
**Alamgirpur** (Uttar Pradesh) on the banks of the river Hindon (a
tributary of Yamuna). \* **Westernmost Point:** **Sutkagendor**
(Balochistan, Pakistan) on the banks of the river Dasht, near the Iran
border.

## 5. TOWN PLANNING AND ARCHITECTURE

Town planning is the most defining feature of the IVC. It shows a level
of civic administration unseen in contemporary civilizations.

### A. The Grid System

- Streets intersected at **right angles** (90 degrees), dividing the
  city into rectangular/square blocks.
- Main streets were aligned North-South and East-West.

### B. Division of the City

Most cities were divided into two parts: 1. **The Citadel (Acropolis /
Raised part):** \* Usually built on the western side. \* Constructed on
an artificially raised platform of mud bricks. \* Housed public
buildings, granaries, and religious/administrative structures. \*
Presumed to be occupied by the ruling/elite class. 2. **The Lower
Town:** \* Usually on the eastern side. \* Much larger in area. \*
Housed the common citizens, merchants, and artisans.

> **Exceptions for Exams:** \* **Dholavira:** Divided into **THREE**
> parts (Citadel, Middle Town, Lower Town). \* **Chanhudaro:** The only
> Indus city with **NO CITADEL**. \* **Lothal:** Citadel and Lower town
> were not separated by an internal wall; both were enclosed by a single
> compound wall.

### C. Building Materials

- Extensive use of **burnt (baked) bricks** (Mesopotamia used sun-dried
  bricks; Egypt used stone).
- Standardized brick ratio: **1 : 2 : 4** (Thickness : Width : Length).
- Use of mud mortar and gypsum (in the Great Bath, bitumen/tar was used
  for waterproofing).

### D. Drainage System

- The IVC had the best drainage system of antiquity.
- Every house had its own courtyard and bathroom connected to the street
  drains.
- Street drains were covered with corbelled stone or bricks and had
  **manholes** at regular intervals for cleaning.

### E. Domestic Architecture

- Houses were 1 or 2 stories high.
- **No windows faced the main streets** (for privacy and dust
  prevention). Doors opened into side alleys.
- Almost every house had a well and a tiled bathroom.

## 6. AGRICULTURE AND ANIMAL HUSBANDRY

### Agriculture

- **Main crops:** Wheat (two varieties) and Barley.
- **Other crops:** Mustard, sesame, peas, jujube, dates, and melons.
- **Cotton:** The Indus people were the **first in the world to produce
  cotton**. The Greeks called it *Sindon* (derived from Sindh).
- **Rice:** Rare. Evidence of rice husk found only at **Lothal** and
  **Rangpur** (Gujarat).
- **Sugarcane:** Unknown to the IVC people.
- **Ploughing:** A terracotta model of a plough was found at
  **Banawali** (Haryana). A ploughed field surface showing criss-cross
  furrows was found at **Kalibangan** (Rajasthan).
- **Irrigation:** Traces of canals found at **Shortughai**
  (Afghanistan), but not in Punjab or Sindh. They relied heavily on
  floodwater. Water reservoirs found at **Dholavira**.

### Animal Husbandry

- **Domesticated animals:** Oxen, buffaloes, goats, sheep, and pigs.
- **Humped Bull:** Most favored and widely worshipped animal.
- **Horse:** Heavily debated. The IVC is generally considered a
  **horse-less civilization**. However, suspicious bones/remains of
  horses were found at **Surkotada** (Gujarat) and a terracotta horse
  figure at Lothal. Still, the horse was not central to their life
  (unlike the Vedic Aryans).
- **Other animals:** Dogs and cats were kept as pets. Elephants and
  rhinos were known to them (depicted on seals).
- **Lion:** Completely **unknown** to the Indus people (though tigers
  were known).

## 7. ECONOMY, TRADE, AND COMMERCE

- **Nature of Economy:** Flourishing urban economy based on agriculture,
  trade, and crafts.
- **Currency:** **No metallic money/coins** were used. Trade was
  conducted via a **Barter System**.
- **Transport:** Bullock carts (terracotta models found) and boats.
- **Weights and Measures:**
  - Made of **Chert** stone, usually cubical in shape.
  - Followed a binary system in lower denominations (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32…
    up to 12,800).
  - The ratio of **16** was the standard (equivalent to 13.64 grams).
    *Legacy: In India, 1 Rupee = 16 Annas until the decimal system.*

### Foreign Trade (Crucial for Mains/Prelims)

Extensive trade existed with Mesopotamia (Sumeria), Oman (Magan), and
Bahrain (Dilmun). \* **Mesopotamian Records:** Akkadian texts (under
King Sargon) refer to trade relations with **Meluhha** (the ancient name
given to the Indus region). \* The texts mention two intermediate
trading stations: **Dilmun** (Bahrain) and **Makan/Magan** (Oman coast).
\* **Imports:** \* Gold: Kolar (Karnataka), Afghanistan, Persia. \*
Silver: Afghanistan, Persia, South India. \* Copper: Khetri mines
(Rajasthan), Baluchistan, Oman. \* Tin: Afghanistan, Central Asia. \*
Lapis Lazuli (Blue gemstone): Shortughai (Afghanistan). \* Steatite:
Iran. \* **Exports:** Agricultural products, cotton goods, terracotta
figurines, pottery, ivory products, copper, and lapis lazuli.

## 8. SOCIETY, DRESS, AND ORNAMENTS

- **Social Stratification:** Though considered relatively egalitarian
  (no palaces or grand temples found), the division of the city into
  Citadel and Lower Town, and differences in burial practices, suggest
  class differences (rulers/merchants vs. laborers).
- **Patriarchal or Matriarchal?** Due to the abundance of Mother Goddess
  terracotta figurines, many scholars believe the society was
  **Matriarchal** (female-dominated).
- **Dress:** Used cotton and wool. Both men and women wore two pieces of
  cloth.
- **Ornaments:** Worn by **both men and women** (necklaces, fillets,
  armlets, finger-rings). Women exclusively wore girdles, earrings, and
  anklets. Made of gold, silver, copper, bronze, and semi-precious
  stones.
- **Cosmetics:** Cinnabar (lipstick/sindoor), face paint, and collyrium
  (kajal) were known. Bronze mirrors and ivory combs were widely used.
  (A vanity case was found at Harappa).
- **Weapons:** Axes, spears, daggers, bows, and arrows made of copper
  and bronze. **Swords were unknown.** *They were generally peace-loving
  people; defensive weapons are rare.*

## 9. RELIGION AND BELIEF SYSTEMS

- **NO TEMPLES:** Unlike Egypt and Mesopotamia, no temples or dedicated
  religious structures have been found in the IVC (except the Great
  Bath, used for ritual bathing).
- **Mother Goddess:** Chief female deity. A terracotta figure shows a
  plant growing out of a woman’s embryo, representing the Goddess of
  Earth (Fertility).
- **Pashupati Mahadeva (Proto-Shiva):**
  - Chief male deity.
  - Found on a steatite seal in Mohenjo-Daro.
  - Depicted sitting in a yogic posture with three faces and two horns.
  - Surrounded by four animals: **Elephant, Tiger, Rhino, and Buffalo**
    (ETRB).
  - Two deer appear at his feet.
- **Animal Worship:** The **Unicorn** (a mythical one-horned animal) was
  the most worshipped. The Humped Bull was also sacred.
- **Tree/Nature Worship:** The **Pipal tree** was worshipped.
- **Idolatry:** Idolatry was practiced (evident from terracotta
  figurines), but in a domestic setting.
- **Fire Worship:** Fire altars have been found at **Kalibangan** and
  **Lothal**.
- **Superstitions:** Amulets were found in large numbers, suggesting a
  belief in evil spirits/magic.

### Burial Practices

- **Mohenjo-Daro:** Complete burial and post-cremation burial.
- **Harappa:** Extended inhumation in wooden coffins (R-37 cemetery and
  H-cemetery).
- **Lothal:** **Double Burial** (male and female buried together in a
  single grave).
- **Kalibangan:** Circular and rectangular graves.
- **Ropar:** A dog buried with a human master (similar to Neolithic
  Burzahom).
- *Note:* The dead were usually buried in the **North-South**
  orientation, along with pottery and jewelry, indicating a belief in
  life after death.

## 10. ART, CRAFT, AND METALLURGY

### A. Terracotta (Baked Clay)

- Used for making toys, animal figures, and Mother Goddess statues.
- IVC terracotta was generally crude and hand-modeled.

### B. Bronze Sculpture

- Made using the **Lost Wax Technique** (Cire Perdue).
- Most famous example: **The Bronze Dancing Girl** found at Mohenjo-Daro
  (naked, wearing bangles on her left arm, right hand on hip, standing
  in a tribhanga posture).

### C. Stone Sculpture

- **Bearded Priest King:** Made of steatite (soapstone), found at
  Mohenjo-Daro. Wears a shawl decorated with a trefoil (three-leaf)
  pattern. Indicates Mesopotamian influence.
- **Red Sandstone Male Torso:** Found at Harappa. Shows realistic
  rendering of human anatomy.

### D. Seals

- The greatest artistic creation of the IVC.
- Mostly made of **Steatite** (soft stone).
- Usually square (standard size 2 × 2 inches), but rectangular and
  circular seals also existed.
- Purpose: Mainly commercial (used as stamps for trade goods) and as
  amulets.
- Most feature an animal (unicorn, bull) and a short inscription.

### E. Pottery

- Predominantly **Red and Black pottery** (Red background with black
  painted designs).
- Wheel-made pottery.
- Designs included trees, birds, animal figures, and geometrical
  patterns.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 11. SCRIPT AND LANGUAGE

- The Harappan script is **Pictographic** (each sign represents a word
  or an idea) and **Logosyllabic**.
- It has **NOT been deciphered yet**.
- Number of signs: Roughly 400.
- **Direction of writing:** Usually Right to Left.
- **Boustrophedon style:** If the inscription was longer than one line,
  it was written Right to Left on the first line, Left to Right on the
  second, and so on.
- The longest inscription found contains 26 signs.

## 13. DECLINE OF THE CIVILIZATION

The IVC did not disappear overnight. It was a gradual decline from 1900
BCE onwards. The exact cause is still debated, and scholars have
proposed multiple theories. No single cause explains the decline across
the entire vast region.

1.  **Aryan Invasion Theory (Now mostly rejected):**
    - *Proponent:* Mortimer Wheeler, Gordon Childe.
    - *Argument:* Rig Veda mentions *Purandara* (Indra, the destroyer of
      forts). Skeletons found at Mohenjo-Daro suggest a massacre.
    - *Rebuttal:* Skeletons belong to different time periods. Aryan
      arrival (1500 BCE) has a massive time gap with IVC decline (1900
      BCE).
2.  **Devastating Floods:**
    - *Proponent:* Ernest Mackay, Sir John Marshall.
    - *Argument:* Mohenjo-Daro and Lothal show massive flood silt
      layers.
3.  **Tectonic Movements / Earthquakes:**
    - *Proponent:* Robert Raikes, George Dales.
    - *Argument:* Upliftment of the earth’s crust caused the Indus river
      to change course, leading to flooding or drying up of cities.
4.  **Drying up of the Ghaggar-Hakra River:**
    - *Proponent:* D.P. Agrawal, Yash Pal.
    - *Argument:* Tectonic shifts caused Sutlej and Yamuna to change
      course, drying up the Saraswati river system where most cities
      were located.
5.  **Ecological Imbalance / Climate Change (Most accepted theory
    today):**
    - *Proponent:* Walter Fairservis, Shireen Ratnagar.
    - *Argument:* Over-exploitation of resources (excessive
      deforestation for baking bricks, overgrazing) combined with a
      shift in the monsoon pattern (aridity) led to agricultural
      collapse and mass migration toward the East (UP/Bihar) and South
      (Gujarat).

## 14. LEGACY AND SURVIVAL

Though urban structures collapsed, the *culture* survived and permeated
into later Indian traditions: \* The worship of Shiva (Pashupati) and
Linga/Yoni worship. \* Reverence for the Pipal tree and sacred animals
(Bull). \* Use of amulets, talismans, and magic (mirrored in the Atharva
Veda). \* The standardized weight system (binary) influenced later
Indian coinage. \* Binary town planning (Vastu Shastra roots). \* The
use of kajal, vermillion (sindoor), and bangles by Indian women.

## 16. PREVIOUS YEAR QUESTIONS (PYQ) THEMES & TRAPS

- **TRAP 1:** *Statement: “The Harappans used iron weapons for
  warfare.”*
  - **FALSE:** Iron was unknown to them. They used copper/bronze, and
    they were mostly peaceful.
- **TRAP 2:** *Statement: “Temples were the center of Harappan
  administration.”*
  - **FALSE:** No temples have been found anywhere in the IVC.
- **TRAP 3:** *Statement: “Coins were the primary medium of exchange.”*
  - **FALSE:** Barter system was used. Seals were for authentication,
    not currency.
- **TRAP 4:** *Statement: “Horses were widely used in agriculture and
  chariots.”*
  - **FALSE:** Horse remains are highly disputed (found only at
    Surkotada in late phases); they did not use horse-drawn chariots
    (that was the Aryans).
- **TRAP 5:** *Matching Sites with Rivers:* Always remember: Harappa is
  on **Ravi** (not Indus), Mohenjo-Daro is on **Indus**, Kalibangan is
  on **Ghaggar**, Lothal is on **Bhogava**.
